Foundations of Motion

This section breaks down the essential terminology used to describe motion. Understanding these core ideas is the first step to mastering the physics of how things move.

Distance vs. Displacement

While they might seem similar, these two quantities tell very different stories about an object's journey. Distance is the total path covered, while displacement is the shortest straight-line path from start to finish.

The dashed blue line is the total distance traveled, while the solid red line is the final displacement.

Speed vs. Velocity

Speed is a scalar quantity that tells you how fast an object is moving. Velocity is a vector quantity that tells you not only the speed but also the direction of motion.

Speed

It is the rate of change of distance. A car traveling at 60 km/h has a speed of 60 km/h.

Velocity

It is the rate of change of displacement. A car traveling at 60 km/h due east has a velocity of 60 km/h east.

Uniform Circular Motion

Motion in a circle presents a unique case. Even if the speed is constant, the object is always accelerating because its direction of velocity is continuously changing.

Visualizing Circular Motion

The animation shows an object moving at a constant speed in a circle. Notice how the velocity vector (the arrow) is always tangent to the path. This change in direction, even without a change in speed, means there is acceleration, called centripetal acceleration, directed towards the center of the circle.

The speed (v) can be calculated using the circumference (2πr) and the time period (T) for one revolution:

v = (2 * π * r) / T
